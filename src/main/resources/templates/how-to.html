How to write an engaging story

The story is a core component of the app and needs to be equally compelling and informative. It should give a good introduction to a topic of choice, e.g.,

  • History: Make students relive historical situations, or teach them about important events from an immersive perspective
  • Ethics: Pose ethical dilemmas to your students and discuss the results in class
  • Economics: Write a branching story that shows students how certain decisions, such as raising the base interest rate, affects daily live

From our own personal experience, we derived a 6-step guide on how to write an engaging story:

  1. Don't give too much thought about an overly specific topic. It is easier to embed your story in one big background, such as World War I, instead of, for example, the Yalta Conference.
  2. Make clear in advance which facts you want to convey. Resources like Lemo, bpb or Wikipedia can help you to get an initial overview. Don't overdo it with the facts, your story shouldn't be a mere list of historical events in the end
  3. Think of a framing storyline. It's best to divide the story into acts or scenes, and arrange them into an arc of tension. Be inspired by things you've read, heard or seen lately. TV series in particular have mastered the art of keeping up tension.
  4. Add your facts from your earlier research to the scenes and think about which emotions fit. Write a short exposé for each scene. This helps you to visualize the plotline. If you have a partner, ask him or her to help you write the dialogues. A conversation can be better conceived in pairs if different roles can be assumed. Dialogue is not a must, though. You can write everything from a ferst person-view and describe the thought world of the protagonist.
  5. If you have multiple scenes with a stringent story, you can think about how to build different story branches. Paint the story as a tree of decisions to keep track of the story (and help you connecting them later in the interface). Limit yourself to two options per story block, otherwise, the tree branches too far out and it's hard to keep track of the different paths. Let the different options end up int he same end again. This way you can ensure that everyone has the same information at the end. With more preparation time you can also build sophisticated branched story
  6. Read everything again at the end and be honest with yourself if you enjoy following the story.

Don't be afraid to ask for help. Not everyone is born a Hemingway, but as a teacher you should learn something new every day. If you need a primer to get started on writing, read this article from The Guardian, which gives tips to aspiring writers. Also, if you should take a liking to writing fiction, take a look at Stephen King's Twenty Rules for Writers—he didn't become the Master of Suspense for nothing.
One more tip: creating a new story block whenever you usually want to create a new paragraph makes your story more immersive. Just create a block where both reactions lead to the same next ID – boom! An illusion of choice is created.

Further resources

Here are some more resources for all your writing needs:

Photos and pictures

  • Pexels is an excellent resource for CC0-licensed pictures which you are free to use in any way you like
  • TheStocks is a page where you can find a myriad of photos, graphics and illustrations. Not all of them are CC0, though, so be sure to check the license!

Writing and planning

  • On Writing Well is a great book which guides you not only through the process of writing, but also through research for your texts
  • Stephen King's A Memoir of the Craft is a great read and full of tips on how to improve your writing style
  • Draw.io is a tool which helps you to create flow charts, so you won't lose track of your story. We personally prefer pen and paper, but maybe a software might be the better approach of you're constantly changing the names of your blocks
